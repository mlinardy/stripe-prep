📖 Pricing Analyst Glossary

1. Volume & Scale Metrics

GMV Gross Merchandise Value

Total dollar value of goods/services sold through a platform or marketplace, before fees, refunds, or returns are deducted.

Example: A marketplace built on Stripe Connect does $50M in GMV this quarter. Stripe doesn't earn revenue on all $50M — only on the portion it processes, multiplied by its take rate.

TPV Total Payment Volume

The total dollar amount of transactions processed through Stripe's payment rails — Stripe's equivalent of "throughput." The base on which take rate is calculated.

Example: Stripe processed $1.4T in TPV in 2024 (+38% YoY), generating ~$5.1B in net revenue — a ~0.36% blended take rate.

Authorization Rate

The % of payment attempts that are approved by the card network/issuing bank. Higher authorization rates mean more successful transactions without any change in pricing.

Example: Hertz saw a 4% increase in authorization rates after moving to Stripe — this directly grows TPV (and thus revenue) with zero price change, a "value-based" growth lever.

Active Accounts / Active Merchants

The number of merchants or end-users actively transacting on the platform in a given period — the denominator for per-merchant revenue metrics.

Example: PayPal reports 426M active accounts (391M consumer + 35M merchant) — a scale metric used to compare network size across competitors.

2. Revenue & Profitability Metrics

Net Revenue

Revenue Stripe retains after passing through interchange fees and card network costs to issuing banks and card networks. This is the "real" revenue line for take-rate calculations.

Example: Stripe's 2024 net revenue was ~$5.1B on $1.4T of TPV processed — the rest of the gross fee collected flows through to banks/networks as interchange.

Gross Revenue / Gross Profit

Gross revenue = total fees charged to customers before costs. Gross profit = revenue minus direct costs of delivering the service (e.g., processing costs, interchange, infrastructure).

Example: Block reported ~$22.1B gross revenue but ~$8.9B gross profit in 2024 — gross profit is the more comparable "apples to apples" metric across payment companies with different fee structures.

Take Rate

Net revenue ÷ payment volume processed, usually expressed in basis points (bps) or as a %. The core "how much do we keep per dollar processed" metric for a payments Pricing Analyst.

Example: Stripe's blended take rate is ~36 bps (0.36%) vs. Adyen's ~16 bps — Stripe captures more per dollar processed, partly via attach-rate of add-on products.

Basis Points (bps)

A unit equal to 1/100th of a percentage point (1 bps = 0.01%). Used because take rates are very small percentages and "bps" avoids awkward decimals.

Example: "Our take rate moved from 34 bps to 36 bps" is easier to discuss than "0.34% to 0.36%."

Effective Rate vs. List Price / Price Realization

List price = the published headline rate (e.g., 2.9% + $0.30). Effective rate = the actual average rate a customer pays after volume discounts/negotiation. Price realization = effective rate ÷ list price, showing how much of the "sticker price" is actually captured.

Example: An enterprise merchant negotiates down from the 2.9%+30¢ list price to an effective ~2.2% — a price realization of roughly 75%.

Contribution Margin / Unit Economics

Revenue per transaction (or per customer) minus the variable costs to serve it — e.g., interchange, fraud losses, infrastructure, support. Used to check whether a pricing tier or discount is still profitable.

Example: Before approving a large volume discount, a Pricing Analyst checks whether the contribution margin on that customer's transactions stays positive after interchange and fraud costs.

Interchange Fee

A fee paid to the card-issuing bank on every card transaction, set by card networks (Visa, Mastercard, etc.) — NOT set by Stripe. Stripe passes this cost through (bundled into its flat rate or itemized in interchange-plus pricing).

Example: Within Stripe's 2.9%+30¢ rate, a large portion is interchange that flows to the cardholder's bank — Stripe's actual markup is much smaller than the headline rate suggests.

Float Income / Interest Income

Revenue earned from interest on customer funds held temporarily before payout (e.g., Stripe Treasury balances). Highly sensitive to prevailing interest rates.

Example: When interest rates rise, float income on held balances increases — but this isn't "pricing" revenue and must be modeled separately from take-rate-driven revenue.

3. Per-Customer / Unit Economics

ARPU / Revenue per Merchant

Average Revenue Per User (or Merchant) = total net revenue ÷ number of active merchants over a period. Tracks how "deeply monetized" the customer base is — a key metric for attach-rate and upsell strategy.

Example: If revenue per merchant rises while merchant count stays flat, it signals successful upsell/attach (more merchants adopting Billing, Tax, Radar, etc.) rather than just new-logo growth.

ACV Annual Contract Value

The annualized dollar value of a customer's contract — common in enterprise, sales-led pricing where deals are negotiated rather than self-serve.

Example: A large platform signs a 3-year deal worth $9M total — its ACV is $3M, used to compare deal sizes across the enterprise sales pipeline.

CAC Customer Acquisition Cost

Total sales & marketing spend divided by the number of new customers acquired in a period. Used alongside LTV to judge whether acquisition spend is sustainable.

Example: If Stripe spends $50M on sales/marketing and acquires 10,000 new paying merchants, CAC = $5,000 per merchant.

LTV Customer Lifetime Value

The total net revenue (or profit) expected from a customer over the entire relationship, factoring in retention/churn and expected attach/upsell over time.

Example: A merchant with $2M/year in TPV and a 36 bps take rate generates ~$7,200/year — multiplied by an expected 8-year lifespan, LTV ≈ $57,600 (before discounting).

LTV:CAC Ratio

The ratio of customer lifetime value to acquisition cost. A common rule of thumb is that a ratio above 3:1 indicates healthy, sustainable growth economics.

Example: LTV of $57,600 vs. CAC of $5,000 gives an LTV:CAC ratio of ~11.5:1 — comfortably above the 3:1 benchmark.

Payback Period

The time it takes for the cumulative margin/revenue from a customer to recoup the cost of acquiring them (CAC).

Example: If a merchant generates ~$600/month in net revenue and CAC was $5,000, the payback period is just over 8 months.

4. Growth & Retention Metrics

Attach Rate

The % of customers using a core product who also adopt an additional product (e.g., the % of Payments customers who also use Billing, Tax, or Radar). One of the most important levers for growing revenue per merchant without changing core pricing.

Example: If 40% of Payments customers also use Stripe Tax, raising that attach rate to 50% directly increases blended take rate — a classic Pricing Analyst growth initiative.

Upsell

Increasing revenue from an existing customer by moving them to a higher-priced tier, plan, or usage level (e.g., from Billing to Billing Scale, or from standard Radar to Radar for Fraud Teams).

Example: A merchant on standard Radar ($0.05/screen) upgrades to Radar for Fraud Teams ($0.07/screen) for better ML-driven fraud detection — an upsell within the same product family.

Cross-sell

Selling a different (adjacent) product to an existing customer, rather than upgrading their current product. Distinct from attach rate in that it's often initiated by a sales/marketing motion rather than self-serve adoption.

Example: A Payments customer is cross-sold Stripe Capital for business financing — a different product line entirely, but enabled by the existing payments relationship and data.

NRR / NDR Net Revenue (Dollar) Retention

The % of recurring revenue retained AND expanded from an existing customer cohort over a year, including upsells, cross-sells, downgrades, and churn. NRR > 100% means expansion outweighs losses.

Example: If a $10M cohort of merchants generates $11.2M one year later (after some churned and others expanded usage), NRR = 112% — a strong sign that attach/upsell strategy is working.

GRR Gross Revenue Retention

Like NRR, but EXCLUDING any expansion/upsell — it measures pure retention (churn and downgrades only), capped at 100%. The gap between GRR and NRR shows how much of retained growth comes from expansion vs. just "not losing" customers.

Example: GRR of 92% with NRR of 112% means churn/downgrades cost 8 points, but upsell/expansion added back 20 points — expansion is the dominant growth driver.

Churn Rate (Logo vs. Revenue)

Logo churn = % of customers (accounts) lost in a period. Revenue churn = % of revenue lost from departing/downgrading customers. The two can diverge significantly (e.g., losing many small customers but little revenue).

Example: Losing 5% of merchant accounts (mostly very small ones) might only translate to 1% revenue churn if those accounts contributed little volume.

Cohort Analysis

Tracking groups of customers acquired in the same period (a "cohort") over time to see how their revenue, usage, or retention evolves — often used to validate whether a pricing change improved or hurt long-term value.

Example: Comparing the revenue trajectory of merchants onboarded before vs. after a Billing price change shows whether the new pricing improved attach rate and retention for the newer cohort.

5. Pricing Models & Strategy

Interchange-Plus Pricing

A pricing structure where the merchant pays the actual interchange + network fees (pass-through, fully itemized) plus a separate, transparent markup. More transparent but more complex than flat-rate pricing.

Example: Adyen prices this way — large enterprises can see exactly what goes to the card networks vs. what Adyen keeps, which builds trust but requires more sophistication to evaluate.

Flat-Rate / Blended Pricing

A single all-in rate charged regardless of card type, region, or interchange variation (e.g., 2.9% + $0.30). Simple for merchants to understand, but Stripe's actual margin varies transaction-by-transaction depending on the underlying interchange cost.

Example: Stripe's headline 2.9%+30¢ rate is the same whether a customer pays with a low-interchange debit card or a high-interchange rewards credit card — Stripe absorbs the variance.

Tiered Pricing

Charging different rates based on product tier, account type, or usage level. Common in SaaS and platform products.

Example: Stripe Connect has Standard (no extra platform fee), Express, and Custom account tiers, each with different monthly per-account fees ($2/active account for Express/Custom).

Volume-Based Discounting

Reducing the effective rate as a customer's processing volume increases — typically negotiated for large/enterprise accounts. A core mechanism behind the gap between list price and effective rate.

Example: A merchant processing $500M/year might negotiate a rate well below the 2.9%+30¢ list price, since Stripe's marginal cost per dollar processed declines at scale.

Usage-Based / Metered Pricing

Price scales directly with consumption — per API call, per screened transaction, per seat-month, etc. Increasingly important for AI-native and API-first businesses.

Example: Stripe Radar charges $0.05 per screened transaction (standard) or $0.07 (advanced) — price scales linearly with the number of transactions screened, not a flat subscription.

Value-Based Pricing

Setting price based on the value delivered to the customer (e.g., incremental revenue generated), rather than purely on cost-to-serve.

Example: Stripe's Optimized Checkout Suite helped Turo capture $114M in additional annual revenue — pricing for such a feature can be partially justified by the value created, not just Stripe's cost to build it.

Cost-Plus Pricing

Price = cost to deliver the service + a fixed margin. Simple and defensible, but can leave money on the table for high-value features, or overprice low-value ones.

Example: Pricing Stripe Tax purely as "cost of compliance infrastructure + X% margin" rather than considering the value of avoiding tax penalties for the merchant.

Bundling / Unbundling

Bundling = combining multiple services into a single price (simplicity, but less price discrimination). Unbundling = breaking products into separate, à-la-carte priced add-ons (more granular monetization, but more complex billing).

Example: Stripe bundles interchange + network fees + markup into one flat rate (bundling), but sells Billing, Tax, and Radar as separate add-on fees (unbundling) — a hybrid strategy.

Freemium

Offering a free base tier to drive adoption and reduce friction, monetizing later via premium features, higher usage tiers, or add-ons.

Example: Stripe Atlas charges a low flat fee (~$500) for company incorporation — a near-loss-leader that funnels new startups into the broader Stripe ecosystem where they'll pay processing fees.

Price Elasticity of Demand

A measure of how much demand (transaction volume) changes in response to a price change. High elasticity = small price increases cause large volume drops; low elasticity = customers are relatively price-insensitive.

Example: Raising the per-transaction fee for a small SMB merchant (high elasticity, many alternatives) might cause significant churn, while raising it for an enterprise deeply integrated into Stripe's APIs (low elasticity, high switching cost) may have minimal impact.

Willingness to Pay (WTP)

The maximum price a customer would be willing to pay for a product or feature, often estimated through surveys (e.g., Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter), conjoint analysis, or A/B price tests.

Example: Before launching a new "AI fraud detection" tier, a Pricing Analyst might survey merchants to estimate WTP and set the $0.07/screen price for Radar for Fraud Teams accordingly.

A/B Testing / Price Experimentation

Testing different prices, fee structures, or packaging on different customer segments (often randomized) to measure the causal impact on conversion, revenue, and churn before a full rollout.

Example: Testing a 0.4% vs. 0.5% Billing fee on two random samples of new signups in a specific region, then comparing attach rate and revenue per merchant after 6 months.

Price Discrimination / Segmentation

Charging different prices to different customer segments based on differences in willingness to pay, cost-to-serve, or competitive dynamics — common and legal in B2B (unlike many consumer contexts).

Example: SMBs pay the standard 2.9%+30¢ list price, while large enterprises negotiate custom interchange-plus contracts — both are "fair" prices for very different cost structures and competitive alternatives.

Anchoring

A psychological pricing tactic where presenting a higher reference price first makes the actual price seem more reasonable by comparison.

Example: Showing "Radar for Fraud Teams: $0.07/screening" next to "Standard Radar: $0.05/screening" anchors the perceived value of the premium tier.

6. Payments-Specific & Risk Terms

Chargeback / Dispute Rate

The % of transactions disputed by cardholders (claiming fraud, non-delivery, etc.). High dispute rates can trigger penalties or account restrictions from card networks — a cost that pricing/risk teams must factor in.

Example: A merchant with a 1.5% dispute rate (above the ~1% network threshold) may face additional monitoring fees — Stripe's risk-based pricing/underwriting must account for this.

Fraud Rate

The % of transactions identified as fraudulent. Stripe Radar exists specifically to reduce this — but fraud prevention pricing must balance catching fraud against blocking legitimate sales.

Example: Aggressive Radar rules might cut fraud rate from 0.3% to 0.1%, but if false declines rise from 1% to 3% of legitimate transactions, the net revenue impact could be negative.

False Decline Rate

The % of legitimate transactions incorrectly blocked by fraud rules — a hidden cost of overly aggressive fraud prevention, since it directly reduces merchant revenue (and thus Stripe's take-rate base).

Example: Turo's $114M revenue gain via Stripe's Optimized Checkout Suite was partly about reducing false declines and improving authorization rates, not just fraud reduction.

Platform Fee / Application Fee

A fee that a software platform or marketplace charges its own sellers, layered on top of Stripe's processing fee — enabled by Stripe Connect's "fee on a fee" model.

Example: A marketplace charges sellers a 5% platform fee plus passes through Stripe's 2.9%+30¢ processing fee — Stripe earns its cut regardless, while the platform monetizes the marketplace relationship.

Revenue Share

An arrangement where revenue from a transaction or product is split between Stripe and a partner — e.g., a bank partner (Treasury) or card issuer (Issuing) sharing interchange income.

Example: In Stripe Issuing, Stripe earns a portion of interchange generated when an issued card is used — shared with the issuing bank partner per their agreement.

Cross-Border Fee / FX Markup

Additional fees (often ~+1% each) charged for transactions involving international cards or currency conversion — a major driver of why "effective" take rates exceed the headline domestic rate.

Example: A US merchant's headline rate is 2.9%+30¢, but a sale to an international customer paying in a foreign currency could effectively cost ~4.9% once cross-border (+1%) and FX conversion (+1%) fees apply.

7. Market Sizing & Competitive Strategy

TAM / SAM / SOM

TAM (Total Addressable Market) = the entire market opportunity. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) = the portion reachable given your business model/geography. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) = the realistic share you can capture given competition and resources.

Example: TAM = all global digital payment volume; SAM = volume in markets/currencies Stripe supports; SOM = the share Stripe can realistically win given Adyen, PayPal, and others competing for the same merchants.

Market Share

The % of total market volume or revenue captured by a given company, used to benchmark competitive position and growth potential.

Example: Stripe holds roughly 20-29% of the global online payment processing market, vs. PayPal's ~43% — together the two control ~65-75% of the market.

Competitive Benchmarking

Comparing your pricing, take rate, and product offerings against competitors to identify where you're over/under-priced or under/over-featured relative to the market.

Example: Comparing Stripe's ~36 bps blended take rate to Adyen's ~16 bps highlights that Stripe monetizes more per dollar of volume — partly justified by a broader add-on product suite (Billing, Tax, Radar) that Adyen's single-platform model doesn't replicate as aggressively.
